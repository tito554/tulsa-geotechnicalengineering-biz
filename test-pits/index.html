The soil profile changes dramatically from one side of Tulsa to the other. Over by the Arkansas River levees near West 21st Street, you might hit sandy alluvium at three feet; head east toward the limestone benches around Turkey Mountain and you'll find weathered shale and chert gravel barely a foot down. That difference matters when you're placing a foundation or planning a stormwater infiltration system. An exploratory test pit lets us see that boundary in full scale, right where it counts. We open the ground, log the strata per ASTM D2488, and pull undisturbed samples for lab work like grain-size or Atterberg limits, all within a city that sits in Seismic Design Category A but still deals with expansive clay cycles that can heave a slab in two seasons. Tulsa's average annual rainfall of about 42 inches, with concentrated spring storms, means surface drainage and soil reactivity are always part of the conversation.

A test pit turns a subsurface guess into a measured section: you see the clay, you feel the moisture, and you log the exact depth where weathered rock begins.

Approach and scope

Tulsa grew in bursts: the oil boom of the 1920s put dense masonry buildings on the shallow bedrock of downtown, while postwar suburbs spread across old pastureland with deeper, more plastic soils. That legacy means we routinely open test pits in backyards where the 'topsoil' is actually redistributed fill from 1960s grading, sitting on a residual clay weathered from Pennsylvanian shale. Our excavation reaches depths usually between 6 and 14 feet, depending on the target formation and the reach of the backhoe. Inside the pit we measure in-situ density with a sand cone, take block samples for triaxial strength testing when the project involves cut slopes, and document groundwater seepage rates that can't be inferred from a boring log alone. The pit walls reveal root penetration, fracture patterns, and desiccation cracks that drive infiltration behavior. Every stratum gets photographed, described, and tied to an elevation datum so the civil engineer can correlate it with the grading plan.

Local ground factors

A contractor opened a 15-foot-deep excavation near the Pearl District for a mixed-use building without recognizing that the upper eight feet were old alluvial terrace deposits overlying a slick, pre-sheared claystone contact. The pit was sloped at 1H:1V based on a generic 'Type B' assumption, but the actual material at the contact behaved closer to Type C. A small wedge failure occurred overnight after a rain shower, collapsing a section of the adjacent alley pavement and delaying the project six weeks while the shoring design was revised. Tulsa's combination of perched groundwater in terrace sands over low-permeability bedrock creates exactly this kind of interface risk. A properly logged test pit, with a geologist on site classifying the intact shear surfaces, would have flagged the weaker zone before the shoring was installed. The cost of the pit is a fraction of the delay and emergency underpinning.

Related technical services

01

Foundation bearing verification pit

Excavated to the proposed footing elevation plus two feet, with hand-trimmed walls to expose the bearing stratum. We perform pocket penetrometer and torvane tests at the base, collect bulk samples for laboratory Proctor and plasticity index, and offer a signed bearing capacity recommendation referencing IBC Chapter 18.

02

Infiltration & drainage test pit

Pit located at the planned stormwater BMP invert, with a 24-hour monitored soak-away test using a calibrated standpipe. Soil texture, redoximorphic features, and seasonal high groundwater marks are logged to support a geotechnical report for the City of Tulsa stormwater permit.

03

Slope-cut investigation pit

Trenched perpendicular to the proposed cut face to map the soil/rock contact and any relic landslide surfaces. Oriented block samples are taken for direct shear or triaxial testing, and the pit log includes joint set orientations measured with a Brunton compass for kinematic stability analysis.

FAQ

What does an exploratory test pit cost in Tulsa, and what drives the price up or down?

For a standard pit 8 to 12 feet deep, with machine excavation, backfill, and a geologist's field log, the range typically falls between US$470 and US$930. Price moves with access difficulty (tight backyard vs. open lot), depth beyond 12 feet, the need for shoring if a person enters the pit, and the number of samples collected for the lab. Sites east of downtown where bedrock is shallow can be faster and cheaper; deep alluvial sites near the Arkansas River often require more time and spoil handling.

How do you decide between an exploratory test pit and a soil boring with SPT?

We choose a test pit when the primary goal is direct observation of soil structure, fracture patterns, or fill contacts — things a split-spoon sample can miss. A boring with SPT gives you blow counts and continuous depth, but a pit shows you the lateral continuity and lets you take large, relatively undisturbed block samples. In Tulsa, where old alluvial terraces and weathered shale interfaces control slope stability, we often recommend one or two pits to calibrate the boring logs, especially on commercial sites with retaining walls or deep utilities.

What backfill and compaction standard do you follow when closing the test pit?

We backfill in lifts of 8 to 12 inches using the excavated soil, moisture-conditioned as needed, and compacted with a jumping-jack or plate compactor to at least 95% of the maximum dry density from a standard Proctor test (ASTM D698). If the pit is within a future building footprint or pavement area, we can use a lean clay or select fill import and offer density testing results. For pits in landscaped areas, the top six inches are left loose and slightly mounded to allow for natural settlement.
